Raising venture capital is a high-stakes, high-skill game. Most founders approach it backwards — they pitch before they're ready, to the wrong investors, with a weak story. This guide gives you the playbook that actually works.

## Understanding the Funding Stages

| Stage | Amount | What You Need | Investors |
|-------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Pre-Seed | $50k–$500k | Idea + team | Friends, family, angels |
| Seed | $500k–$3M | MVP + early traction | Angel syndicates, micro-VCs |
| Series A | $3M–$20M | Product-market fit + revenue | Institutional VCs |
| Series B | $20M–$100M | Proven scaling model | Growth equity |
| Series C+ | $100M+ | Market leader | Late-stage, pre-IPO |

Most founders should target the stage that matches their current evidence. Raising too early (without proof) leads to rejection or bad terms. Raising too late means you've diluted unnecessarily with bootstrapped capital.

## What Investors Actually Look For

VCs are pattern-matching machines. They've seen thousands of pitches and are looking for specific signals:

**Team** — Can these founders execute? Do they have unfair advantages (domain expertise, network, technical edge)? Have they shown resilience?

**Market** — Is the total addressable market large enough to produce a venture-scale outcome? (Generally requires a $1B+ TAM)

**Traction** — What evidence do you have that people want this? Users, revenue, engagement metrics, or clear leading indicators.

**Differentiation** — Why will you win? What's the moat that prevents competitors from copying you in 6 months?

**Business Model** — How do you make money? Is the unit economics viable (LTV > 3x CAC is typical benchmark)?

:::info
**The Sequoia Pitch Deck Framework:** Purpose → Problem → Solution → Why Now → Market → Product → Team → Business Model → Financials. This structure has guided some of the most successful pitches in Silicon Valley history.
:::

## Building Your Pitch Deck

Your pitch deck is your story in 10–15 slides. Every word should earn its place.

### Slide 1: Company Purpose (1 sentence)
"Stripe is a suite of APIs that lets businesses accept payments online."
Clear, concrete, specific.

### Slide 2: The Problem
Paint a vivid picture of the pain. Use data, stories, or quotes from real customers. If the problem isn't felt immediately, investors mentally check out.

### Slide 3: The Solution
Your product, not your technology. Show the demo if you have it. "Seeing is believing" applies to investor conviction.

### Slide 4: Why Now
Why hasn't this been solved before? What changed — regulatory, technological, behavioral, or market? This answers the "why didn't anyone do this 5 years ago?" objection.

### Slide 5: Market Size
Bottom-up calculation preferred over citing Gartner reports.

```
Example — Online Education Platform:
- Target customer: Working professionals seeking upskilling
- Average annual spend per customer: $600/year
- Total professionals seeking upskilling in US: 25M
- TAM = 25M × $600 = $15B

Serviceable Addressable Market (SOM) Year 5:
- Realistic capture rate: 0.5%
- SAM = 25M × 0.5% × $600 = $75M ARR
```

### Slide 6: Business Model
Revenue streams, pricing, and unit economics (LTV:CAC ratio, gross margins, payback period).

### Slide 7: Traction
This is the most important slide at seed stage. Show any evidence of demand:
- Monthly active users and growth rate
- Revenue (MRR/ARR) and growth curve
- Enterprise LOIs or paid pilots
- Customer testimonials with names

### Slide 8: Product
Screenshots, demo link, or key features. Show, don't tell.

### Slide 9: Team
Why are you the team to win this? Relevant backgrounds, prior exits, domain expertise, advisors. This slide is about credibility and conviction.

### Slide 10: The Ask
How much are you raising? How will you use it? What milestones will it take you to? Be specific.

## The Fundraising Process

### Step 1: Build Your Investor List

Research thoroughly before reaching out. Identify 100–200 investors who:
- Invest at your stage
- Have portfolio companies in adjacent spaces (not competitors)
- Are actively deploying capital

Use Crunchbase, AngelList, and Twitter to research. Find investors who've written about your problem space.

### Step 2: Get Warm Introductions

Cold outreach has <1% success rate. Warm introductions have 20–30% first meeting conversion. Work your network relentlessly to find mutual connections.

### Step 3: Run Parallel Processes

Never pitch one investor at a time. Run all meetings in parallel (ideally within a 6-week window). FOMO is a powerful motivator — investors move faster when they sense others are interested.

### Step 4: Manage the Pipeline

Keep a spreadsheet tracking:
- Investor name, firm, stage
- Introduction source
- Meeting date
- Status (emailed / met / excited / term sheet / passed)
- Notes on concerns to address

### Step 5: Navigate Due Diligence

After a term sheet, investors will verify everything in your deck. Have ready:
- Cap table (equity structure)
- Financial model
- Customer references
- Legal documentation
- Technical documentation

## Term Sheet Essentials

The terms that matter most:

**Valuation:** Pre-money vs post-money matters significantly. $5M pre-money valuation + $1M raise = 20% dilution. Know the math before you sign.

**Pro-rata rights:** Investors' right to maintain their ownership percentage in future rounds. Standard for lead investors.

**Liquidation preference:** 1x non-participating is founder-friendly. Anything above 1x or with participation rights hurts founders in downside scenarios.

**Board seats:** Seed rounds typically don't include board seats. Series A usually gives investors one board seat.

## FAQ

### How long does fundraising take?

Plan for 3–6 months from first pitch to money in the bank. Most founders underestimate this by 2–3x. Start raising 6 months before you need the money.

### Should I raise on a SAFE or priced round?

SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) are faster, cheaper, and simpler for seed rounds under $1M. Priced equity rounds offer more certainty for all parties and are standard at Series A.

### What's a reasonable amount of dilution to accept?

At seed: 15–25% dilution is typical. At Series A: 20–30%. Across multiple rounds, founding teams often end up with 30–50% at IPO. Dilution is acceptable when it funds growth that increases the absolute value of your remaining ownership.
